Notes On Productivity

Latarence Butts
April 03, 2021

My keys to productivity have always been preparation, organization, and prioritization. I’ve carried these with me for as long as I can remember. However, these principles are evolving for me as I manage a larger and more diverse workload. Here are a few notes i've taken.
Preparation. Before beginning work, I typically do my best to clear any obstacles and identify any tools. This entails a range of things from organizing physical work area to setting up the directory where I'll be storing files for a new model. I even familiarize with all of the requirements for a task about a week before I complete the task. On the flip side, I'd say I'm guilty of overpreparation. At times I spend too much time organizing files and folders, clarifying notes here and there, revising to do lists and timelines, etc. In the moment these things feel like a waste of time, especially in the midst of a deadline. Other times I can become frustrated beyond belief when I can’t find things I need. The need for organization increases as I have more projects to balance, or as you take a ton of notes.

I approach my work as a combination of art and engineering. It’s still forming into a comprehensive approach to work. Overall, I would say I take a say by day approach to long term goals. In most cases, I would say that isn’t the way to go about things. Many people can’t survive without having an outside force that dictates.
I tend to look at things 3 ways: with disregard for normality, holistically, with reductionism. You must be selective with when to embrace the status quo as your own belief. History is filled with places where we have been everyone was wrong. We cannot use sheer majority as a guiding compass. Scientific history is populated with much fewer examples of the such because of its objectivity (when done correctly) and its standard for acceptance of truth. I tend to be less inclined to accept findings from behavioral science as truth. Surely this belief is a result of ignorance and bias. It’s just hard to reconcile the complexity and uncontrollability of confounding variables. 
Plainly, one might say that having an outlined schedule is a key to productivity. Well, why? Because you are sure to work at a task? 
What if your productivity is a measure of the originality or usability of the output, instead of the quantity of such input.
As beings we live a according to a rhythm. But are we truly designed to live every second of our life in a premeditated task? Even if you love what you do, that sounds like a very enslaved lifestyle. Where is your free will expressed beyond the desire to follow that schedule?
I tend to place priority on prioritization other than schedule. This may change for me. When I adopted this mentality I was in a very explorative phase. In undergrad I operated according to a schedule that was dictated by the assignments given to me. In an explorative phase, you experiment with different tasks. You gauge the time you spend on them. You observe the impact it has on you and continue to make adjustments. 
Ultimately. The key to productivity is awareness and action. You are unique individual. Create as such. Try a system and measure. We should strive to make meaningful contributions in our time here and to preserve ourselves, but never let our capitalistic society fool you. Life is to be lived. 
Growth tracking is another tool that can enhance productivity. The benefit of this is to measure your productivity over time. In the day to day, I haven’t found it to benefit much.  Many people implement growth tracking for a confidence boost. They need to see small successes to remain optimistic.
Overall, I have a holistic approach to productivity. I value the merit and originality in my work. Many times, this means that I have to be in the mood to certain things. I think that I should have the ability to do these things when the desire arises. I think that this freedom is true wealth. But It takes focus and discipline to acquire and maintain.
Reductionism is the way that I solve problems and another principle that I employ in my approach to productivity.  Reductionism is the method by which I prioritize in many ways. Your long term vision is important in order to maintain context for everything that you do. Having the vision and end goal, the next step for me is to break the requirements down to the necessary components required. This is the reductionism part. Then the parts get prioritized by order of execution and importance.

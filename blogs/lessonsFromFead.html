Lessons From Fead

Latarence Butts
April 03, 2021

What was Fead? The haziness of this story in my mind makes me happy that I’m telling it now.  I don’t recall how exactly I conceptualized Fead, except that I was in the disposition similar to  that described in The History Of Runwae. I wanted to make something meriterious and better prepare for the future. I remember the idea being simple and common sense. 
Fead was the first “real” project that I set out to create. It was the first that would potentially serve people, have some value, structure, etc. To be clear, it wasn’t anything close to a startup. It was a training course that I had set up for myself. 
So, what was it? Fead was a platform for small business to sell their data to each other. Still today, I have no doubt that it’s a great idea. I do think that it was premature and would require some real influence to get going. 
Here was my premise: Big companies sell data to each other under the table all the time. 
Why Was Feed Started I successfully created the platform and did some branding. I gained experience with understanding documentation for development. Setting up a database. Debugging on the web. Setting up a development environment as far as tools. I started on notepad then went to brackets. Actually, my development history is as such:
Sophomore HS: Android App dual enrollment at FAU Junior HS: Matlab Dev Fresh:  Web Notepad, Arduino Soph: Brackets, VHDL, TI Code Composer,  Jun: Vis Studio, Rasp Y1: PyCharm
Why Was Fead Stopped I don’t recall the exact stopping point. The reason for stopping was that we weren’t ready for what we were undertaking. 
The market wasn’t ready for the idea of fead, but fead wasn’t even more unready. I had developed a platform for small businesses to post data in the form of excel documents. Other business could shop on their feed for data posted by small business.
Why I say the market wasn’t ready: Many small businesses didn’t have data collection tools This is no longer true in 2020. Point of sale systems like square create tools that help store owners capture data and understand it.
More than capturing basic data, technology has evolved to integrate platforms to leverage their relative strengths. Now companies with little technical depth can track user behavior, conversions, and market based on their data.
The culture has also evolved. There is a dialogue about the power of data and its value. There is an understanding that numbers should be consulted in strategizing, if not guiding the ship. Why? Numbers don’t lie. Numbers are reliable. Numbers aren’t biased. This is limited to most cases, of course. 
The culture is still not ready in the sense that there is still a misunderstanding of data as it relates to its role and power in society. A small business with numbers doesn’t understand the context of those within society.  I think data will become a commodity and that this will change. I think tech leads this as in most areas.
We are becoming more emotionally intelligent. We will increasingly value the “why” and the  “how” for companies, not just the “what”. Transparency will continuously be valued in companies. Therefore, people will understand that sharing anonymous numbers is ok. 
Overall, the sharing of data is a far greater benefit than it is a risk. Small business have yet to understand this point. They feel that exposing their numbers will harm them. However, in many cases, it just isn’t the case.
Direct competitors may gain an advantage that they wouldn’t have otherwise. However, soon the entire market will be sharing data. This will just be another ingredient in the competition equation.
My goal at the time was to create the platform. Not to create point of sale tools or the data visualization tools, or create the awareness around data that would lead the change in culture.

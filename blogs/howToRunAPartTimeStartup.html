How To Run A Part Time Startup

Latarence Butts
March 27, 2021

Running a startup is a fascinating journey. You get to work closely with peers and make great friends. You change lives with your product and have fun in the process. You get to dream big and shoot for the stars. However, being a part of a startup isn't ideal for everyone. We have, school, work, or other commitmemnts that limit the time that we can contribute. It's not easy, but in the early stages at least, it is possible to get your startup going in the background of some larger commitments. For the better part of three years now, I've been building a startup while working on a PhD in electrical engineering. This piece focuses on the strategy and keys that allowed us to sustain and even grow our startup with limited availability and commitment. Hopefully, this article helps you and your team make some of the adjustments or realizations that are required to reach your mission.

Culture Fit



Accomplishing a significant task with a team of part time workers is not an easy feat. Part time teammates splittheir time, energy, and attention between several assignments and goals. This abnormal work condition requires a different structuring of roles and scheduling of meetings to yield successfule results. Most of all, it requires a strong commonality concerning work philosophies and vision.

At Runwae, the thing we love above all else is to build. We love to build so much in fact, that we each decided to take on a role outside of our full time role, to create something amazing. If your team doesn't share a strong appreciation for the goal being accomplished and a common vision for how to accomplish it, the commitment of your teammates will waiver and attendance will fluctuate. Other responsibilities will take precedence over your meeting attendance. Make sure the end goal is the centerpiece for all communication and encounters. Work encounters are less frequent, so your team must be remnided of the mission often.



Check Ins



In a traditional business or organization, you try to avoid meetings without meaninful updates or announcements because they can interrupt important events or just waste resources. In a part-time effort, these "pointless" meetings are your most prized possession. Here's why: in a traditional business, you encounter your deliverables and evaluate progress everyday. This luxury is nonexistent for part time teams. There are times when our primary jobs take extraordinary efforts, and life takes up everything after that, or vice versa even. In these times, we may not complete, progress, or even see our part-time tasks for an entire week.

A meeting is crucial in this scenario. After that week, the task becomes more and more distant for some people. Our minds push it further down the priority list. A meeting allows teammates to compensate for the lack of progress. It also resets that teammate’s perspective on the tasks. Reminds them how it fits into the bigger picture, Teams and long term tasks require consistency. 



Deadlines 



Deadlines are typically a good idea for the simple fact that they help ensure encountability and allow pgrogress to be planned or analyzed. In an ideal work scenario my team wouldn't have deadlines because work would get done as a healthy pace from natural motivation, and because we would communicate progress well. If you use deadlines, make them significant. Ensure that your team delivers on these deadline or gives notice. This comes down to creating a culture that values dealines. Your team has to understnad the criticality of meeting deadlines to the accomplishing of the ultimate goal. If deadlines aren't met then progess can't be made.

The attitutde of the team toward cancellations or absences will be a significant factor in your teams ability to function part time.

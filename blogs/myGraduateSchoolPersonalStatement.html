Lessons From Gigalyze

Latarence Butts
Jun 5, 2021

Graduating high school with a minor in mathematics, I knew more than anything else that I
wanted to be an engineer. The only issue with this was I never really knew what this career path
entailed. I had never interacted with anyone who was an engineer, so I didn’t have any concept
or basis of what they really were past what common knowledge I had picked up online or from
my teachers. One thing I did know, however, was that I needed to be comfortable with and
efficient in mathematics. Lucky for me this is where my passion stemmed and where my vision
for a career in engineering started to take form. ‍
I’ve always been the type of person that is relentless in my pursuit for success. In high school, I
was named male scholar athlete of the year and ESPN player of the week. I was an all-state
basketball player as a sophomore, and again as a junior, and I am confident that I would have
been again as a senior if I hadn’t fractured both of my shins. This non-impact injury was so
severe and abnormal that the doctors performed a biopsy on both of my shins because the
oncologist, grasping for answers for my unwarranted condition, thought there could be tumors in
my shins. When basketball was taken away from me, I realized that I needed an outlet for the
passion and commitment I once put towards the game and decided that I would use all of that
potential, dedication, and drive in my pursuit of a professional career in engineering. My hard
work will pay off this semester as I will graduate with a Bachelor of science in electrical
engineering after 3 years while dual enrolling for my master of science during the 3rd year.
‍ During my three undergraduate years at Florida A&M University, I’ve been actively involved
multiple student organizations such as Tau Beta Pi in which I currently serve as the chapter vice
president for the fall and spring semesters of the 2017/2018 academic year, and I also served
on the curriculum committee at the 2017 National convention. I’ve also spent my second and
third undergraduate year conducting undergraduate research in the relatively primitive field of
lithium air batteries under the FREEDM Systems Center, supported by the National Science
Foundation. Due to my work with such organizations and through my own academic studies, I
am being honored next month with the Student Leadership Award at the 2018 Black Engineer
of the Year Award STEM Conference. ‍
As an intern with companies such as Northrop Grumman and NextEra energy, I’ve been able to
hold meetings at odd hours of the day with engineers in other parts of the world (some of which
couldn’t disclose their actual location because they were assigned to a black project). I’ve
played pivotal roles on big savings projects in energy, and I’ve written simulations for high
altitude long endurance vehicles that soar undetected. I’ve been afforded all of these
opportunities and more, and I’ve been grateful for the glimpses at what it’s like to be in engineer
that these internships have given me. However, although I have learned about how amazing
fieldwork can be, I have also realized that I have the rest of my life for such work and my
passion for furthering my education is what I want to focus on right now.
‍ During my time in that San Diego cubicle at Northrop Grumman, I sat revising pages of
compatibility documentation that evaluated our compliance to military standards for
electromagnetic interference, and I developed an affinity for telecommunications. During my
time venturing into lithium battery dynamics, I had gained exposure to research and had
become quite thoroughly knowledgeable in this area, but I just knew that energy storage wasn’t
my thing. However, Telecom was something that I could commit to and carried a real desire to
improve upon. The topic was complex, crucial to development, and intriguing. ‍
I hope to return to the FAMU-FSU College of Engineering to complete my master of science
next year. I can honestly say that my academic experience thus far has exceeded my
expectations. I’ve always said that this college of engineering is a trendsetter and leader among
its peers in that it provides a truly integrated and progressive environment that is rarely found
elsewhere. In the Fall of this year, in that same environment, I plan to begin my master thesis on
flexible aerosol jet printed micro strip antennas. I plan to work with a focus on addressing the
shift in resonant frequency across degrees of bending in three-dimensional space.
‍ High school had made me very accustomed to the life of a student athlete. So, when I started
college I needed something in place to balance my perspective and effort. I decided that I would
take on a personal project every year. Plus, it would give me a chance to expanded on practical
knowledge as opposed to the theory that is presented in many courses. More than anything
though, I think I was eager to test my cognitive and creative limits. I wanted to see what I was
capable of. My freshman year I built an elaborate model house of balsa wood. Everything was
sketched in precise dimensions and detailed carefully. Then, I wired the house up with electricity
and turned a DVD remote into a remote controller by hacking the infrared signal. You could
toggle the lights in the different rooms and see the wallpaper through the windows. The chair of
the department of electrical and computer engineering, Dr. Foo, still has it on display in his
office today. ‍
Sophomore year, feeling more learned and advanced after my first internship, I wanted to do
something a bit more ambitious. I had known how to write code for different applications, fairly
well in fact, but I had no exposure to web programming and to learn it thoroughly seemed like a
daunting task. There are so many different tools and platforms out, I didn’t really know where to
start. Still, I decided to take on the challenge. I progressed steadily, and there were many
concepts that I had yet to grasp, but I kept trying. There were many late and frustrating nights,
as well as many breakthrough nights where I would finally solve a huge problem and the feeling
was incredible. I created a platform for small business owners to buy and sell data in an effort to
establish a formal market for data trade as opposed to the “under the table” data trade practiced
by larger companies, and I assembled a group of close friends to pitch ideas and help organize.
By the end of the year it was finished, and in my eyes I felt it was average, I knew that I could
make improvements. Our team assembled a group of students to help test the site and give
feedback on areas of improvement. We gathered the feedback, and I read up on user
experience to get a few pointers. Over the summer, during my free time while interning for
Northrop Grumman in San Diego, I rebuilt the whole site from scratch. The valuable and
accessible feedback from students made them an attractive target audience, one that we
understood. Who knew more about being students than students like ourselves?
‍ Rebuilding the site from scratch was no easy task. For example, In order to allow students to
easily upload documents, I knew that they would need to scan it easily, and most students don’t
have scanners handy. Such tools are mostly built already and accessible online so that they
don’t have to be built from scratch. This web based “camera to scanned pdf API” as it can be
called, did not appear to be online. So, I built one, with no prior knowledge of image processing.
It took me a week, and it may be the hardest thing I’ve ever done, but it works really well and I
am proud of the outcome. Since our official launch in early January, we don’t have as much of a
promising user base as we would like, I know that with more effort towards advertising our user
base can grow. ‍
Lately my interest lies more in discovery, rather than invention. I read The Disappearing Spoon
by Sean Kean and learned about the Marie Curie and Aluminum Boy Wonder. I feel like a kid
again reading about the amazing discoveries that people from different backgrounds made, and
the social repercussions. That feeling of valuable creation is the most precious thing, the genius
of an individual in birthing a concept from nothing. With this in mind, I am leaning towards
pursuing a PhD after finishing my master next year. I want to learn more in depth concepts to
gain a very developed perception of the world around me. However, I’m not one hundred
percent sure because the team spirit and workplace culture in industry is still very tempting. I
hear veterans at Northrop Grumman talk about hectic times they’ve experienced working on
black projects and the relationships they’ve formed and it pulls at me. My desire for discovery is
challenged by tendency for community. While obtaining a master of science, I expect next year
to be a year of much growth in which I will form more precise goals moving forward. ‍
Latarence Butts ‍
